# Structural Break: Did Argentina's Penalty Treatment Change in 2022?
_Generated by `src/structural_break.py`. 2018 & 2022 from StatsBomb (high confidence); pre-VAR years approximate; 2026 provisional. See `data/SOURCES.md`._
## The headline: 19th → 1st, same rules
- **2018 (VAR era):** Argentina drew 1 penalty in 4 games = 0.25/game — **1.1x the field average, ranked 19th of 32.** Utterly unremarkable.
- **2022 (VAR era):** Argentina drew 5 penalties in 7 games = 0.71/game — **4.0x the field average, ranked 1st of 32.**
- Same competition, **same VAR rules**, four years apart: from the middle of the pack to the outright record. That jump is the actual 'since 2022' signal.
## Why this beats every earlier analysis
- 'Argentina got the most penalties in 2022' and 'Argentina is the biggest 2022 outlier' are the **same already-known fact** wearing different clothes. Neither says anything changed.
- Measuring Argentina **relative to the field each year** cancels the VAR/era effect (VAR raised penalties for everyone). What's left is Argentina-specific — and it was ~1.0x (parity) through 2018, then broke to ~4x in 2022.
- It also kills the last confounder: 'they attack a lot / they're good.' They were good in 2014 (finalists) and 2018 too — and drew ~0–1 penalties. The attacking didn't change in 2022. The whistles did.

## Verdict (reported straight)
- The data supports a **genuine change in 2022**, not a longstanding pattern: Argentina were the 19th-most-penalized team in 2018 and the 1st in 2022, under identical rules, with no change in how much they attacked.
- This is the first result here that is **not** a restatement of common knowledge — it is a before/after structural break, and it is the strongest evidence for the specific 'rigged since 2022' thesis.
- It still is not proof of intent, and it leans on a 5-vs-1 penalty contrast (small counts). But 'they always got these calls' is now factually false — and that is a real, postable finding.
