## #1 Exposure model — the 'they just attack more' rebuttal, tested
The obvious objection to the penalty stat is: *Argentina attacked more, so of course they drew more penalties.* The cleanest test is the other finalist — **France played the same 7 games, took MORE shots, and generated essentially EQUAL xG.**
| Metric | Argentina | France | Argentina / France |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shots | 76 | 92 | 0.83x |
| xG | 11.8 | 11.5 | 1.03x |
| Penalties won | 5 | 2 | 2.5x |
| Penalties per game | 0.71 | 0.29 | 2.5x |
| **Penalties per 100 shots** | **6.6** | **2.2** | **3.0x** |
| Penalties per xG | 0.42 | 0.17 | 2.4x |

**The rebuttal fails.** France attacked *more* (92 vs 76 shots) for *equal* xG, yet got 2.5x fewer penalties. On every exposure-adjusted basis — per game, per shot, per xG — Argentina drew **3.0x-2.5x** as many penalties as an equally elite side. The penalties are not a byproduct of raw attacking volume.
**Honest confounder:** Argentina's xG/shot was higher (0.155 vs 0.125) — their chances came from better positions, i.e. more time in dangerous areas. That plausibly earns *some* extra penalties. But a 15% edge in chance quality does not explain a 2.5-3x gap in penalty rate.

## #3 Placebo control — is the method just flagging good teams?
If our outlier test lit up for any strong team, it would be worthless. So we run the identical Poisson test on France — an elite, high-shot, deep-run finalist:

| Team | Penalties | Games | Expected | Fold | p-value | Flagged? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Argentina | 5 | 7 | 1.42 | 3.5x | 0.0151 | **YES** |
| France | 2 | 7 | 1.42 | 1.4x | 0.416 | no |

**The test discriminates.** Same games, same elite level: Argentina is flagged (p = 0.015), France is not (p = 0.42). The anomaly is Argentina-specific, not an artifact of being a finalist. (And historically, no team *ever* got more than 4 penalties in an edition — so the placebo holds across all of World Cup history, not just France.)
## Verdict
- **The thesis survives its toughest test.** Argentina's penalties are not explained by attacking volume (France out-shot them for fewer), and the outlier test does not fire for a matched control.
- **But it stays honest:** they were favored on penalties *only* — and punished on cards. And none of this proves *intent*. It proves an anomaly that the obvious innocent explanations do not account for. That is a real, defensible point — and the right ceiling for what data like this can claim.
