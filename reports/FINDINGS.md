# Findings: Argentina & Referee Treatment (2022 World Cup)
_Generated by `src/analyze.py`. Numbers trace to `data/` + `data/SOURCES.md`._
## The headline stat
- Argentina were awarded **5 of the 23 open-play penalties** in the entire 2022 World Cup — **21.7%** of every penalty in the tournament, to 1 of 32 teams.
## The formal test
Null hypothesis: Argentina drew penalties at the tournament-average rate, adjusted for how many games they played (they reached the final, so 7 games = more chances than a group-stage team). We model penalties per team-match as Poisson.
- Tournament baseline rate: **0.180 penalties per team-match** (23 penalties / 128 team-matches).
- Expected penalties for a 7-game team at that rate: **1.26**.
- Argentina actually got: **5** — about **4.0x** expectation.
- Poisson P(X >= 5 | lambda = 1.26) = **0.0094**.
- **Verdict: statistically significant at alpha = 0.05.**
## The honest counterweight (do not omit this)
- Argentina **conceded 2 penalties** in 7 games (0.29/game vs a 0.18/game baseline) — both in the final. They were not shielded defensively.
- Argentina drew **16 yellow cards** in 7 games and played in the match with the most cards in World Cup history (18 vs Netherlands). A team that referees 'protect' does not lead the tournament in bookings.
- **Confounder:** penalties are earned partly by play style. Argentina attacked, held the ball, and drew fouls in the box. Per-game normalization helps but cannot fully separate 'favoritism' from 'a lot of dangerous attacking touches.'
## What this does and does not prove
- It **does** show Argentina's penalty count was a genuine statistical outlier, not a vibe.
- It **does not**, by itself, prove intent or bias — an outlier can be produced by style of play, deep tournament runs, and small samples. That distinction is the difference between a credible post and a conspiracy screenshot.
